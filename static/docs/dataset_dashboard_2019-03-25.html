Age and Gender

The total number of respondents was 6530. Of these, 3341 were women, 2475 were men, 28 were other, and 49 preferred not to say. The average age of the sample was 20.92 (SD = 7.26).

Race/Ethnicity

The race/ethnicity variable is complex because people can choose as many different ones as they like (e.g., multiracial individuals), so there are many different categories. The majority of participants were White/Caucasian (54.98%); 9.88% were Hispanic/Latino, 8.18% were East or Southeast Asian, 7.69%, were African American/Black, and the remainder chose another race/ethnicity. Our sample contains less Hispanics/Latinos than expected based on the national population, so we should keep this in mind when speaking about the generality of the effects of OpenMind.

Politics

The sample included more Progressives than Conservatives (scale ranged from 1 = Very Progressive/Left to 7 = Very Conservative/Right; M = 3.54, SD = 1.65). The distribution may be seen in the below figure.

Some participants did not identify with the above political orientations. Rather, 164 selected Libertarian/Classical Liberal, 210 preferred not to say, 84 chose Other, and 867 said that they did not know or that they werent political.

Results Summary

Ingroup-Outgroup Polarization

Ingroup-Outgroup polarization is how much people like their own group relative to an opposing group. For this analysis, we determined ingroup and outgroup by matching peoples poltiical identity with the survey questions asking how much they liked people with the same and different political identity. Specifically, if someone identified as liberal, their ingroup liking score was how much they liked liberals and their outgroup liking score was how much they liked conservatives. If someone identified as conservative, their ingroup liking score was how much they liked conservatives and their outgroup liking score was how much they liked liberals.

We found that 39.09% of users were less polarized after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, t(2626) = 26.35, p < 0.001, Cohens d = 0.51.

Affective Polarization

Affective polarization is how much people like progressives relative to conservatives. This is computed by taking the absolute value of the difference in liking for progressives minus liking for conservatives. This is similar to Ingroup-Outgroup polarization, but includes all participants not just participants identifying as progressive or conservative.

We found that 55.23% of users were less affectively polarized after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, t(4301) = 26.3, p < 0.001, Cohens d = 0.4.

Liking for Outgroup

For this analysis, we determined outgroup by matching peoples poltiical identity with the survey questions asking how much they liked people with the same and different political identity. Specifically, if someone identified as liberal, their outgroup liking score was how much they liked conservatives. If someone identified as conservative, their outgroup liking score was how much they liked liberals.

We found that 36.78% of users liked the political group more (or, disliked them less) after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, t(2626) = 23.06, p < 0.001, Cohens d = 0.45.

Intellectual Humility

For this analysis, we computed the average score of the four survey questions assessing intellectual humility.

We found that 58.96% of users were more intellectually humble after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, t(4307) = 23.57, p < 0.001, Cohens d = 0.36.

Perspective-Taking

We found that 45.84% of users scored higher on perspective-taking after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, t(4306) = 18.26, p < 0.001, Cohens d = 0.28.

Classroom Freedom of Expression

We found that 29.48% of users felt freer to express their beliefs in class after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, t(484) = 0.93, p > 0.10, Cohens d = 0.04.

Growth Mindset

We found that 45.23% of users scored higher on Growth Mindset after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, t(4309) = 16.61, p < 0.001, Cohens d = 0.25.

  xts(x=variables, order.by = date)

hc_add_om <- function(hc, data = hc_dat, 
                      usertype, title, 
                      color, plot_type = "area", y_axis = "n", ...) {
  
  if (usertype == "all") {
    
  hc_plot <- hc %>% 
    hc_add_series(
      make_xts(data %>% pull(y_axis), 
               data %>% pull(MonthYear)), 
      name = title, 
      type = plot_type, 
      color = color,
      ...
      ) 
    
    return(hc_plot)
  }
  
  hc %>% 
    hc_add_series(
      make_xts(data %>% 
                 filter(UserType %in% usertype) %>% 
                 pull(y_axis), 
               data %>% 
                 filter(UserType %in% usertype) %>% 
                 pull(MonthYear)), 
      name = title, 
      type = plot_type, 
      color = color,
      ...
  )   
}

### Results Summary

**Ingroup-Outgroup Polarization**

Ingroup-Outgroup polarization is how much people like their own group relative to an opposing group. For this analysis, we determined "ingroup" and "outgroup" by matching people's poltiical identity with the survey questions asking how much they liked people with the same and different political identity. Specifically, if someone identified as liberal, their ingroup liking score was how much they liked liberals and their outgroup liking score was how much they liked conservatives. If someone identified as conservative, their ingroup liking score was how much they liked conservatives and their outgroup liking score was how much they liked liberals.


We found that `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q17", moderates == "WithoutModerates") %>% pull(percentimproved)`% of users were less polarized after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q17", moderates == "WithoutModerates") %>% pull(tlab)`, Cohen's d = `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q17", moderates == "WithoutModerates") %>% pull(cohend) %>% round(2)`.

**Affective Polarization**

Affective polarization is how much people like progressives relative to conservatives. This is computed by taking the absolute value of the difference in liking for progressives minus liking for conservatives. This is similar to Ingroup-Outgroup polarization, but includes all participants -- not just participants identifying as progressive or conservative. 

We found that `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q14", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(percentimproved)`% of users were less affectively polarized after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q14", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(tlab)`, Cohen's d = `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q14", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(cohend) %>% round(2)`.


**Liking for Outgroup**

For this analysis, we determined "outgroup" by matching people's poltiical identity with the survey questions asking how much they liked people with the same and different political identity. Specifically, if someone identified as liberal, their outgroup liking score was how much they liked conservatives. If someone identified as conservative, their outgroup liking score was how much they liked liberals.


We found that `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q16", moderates == "WithoutModerates") %>% pull(percentimproved)`% of users liked the political group more (or, disliked them less) after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q16", moderates == "WithoutModerates") %>% pull(tlab)`, Cohen's d = `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q16", moderates == "WithoutModerates") %>% slice(1) %>% pull(cohend) %>% round(2)`.

**Intellectual Humility**

For this analysis, we computed the average score of the four survey questions assessing intellectual humility.


We found that `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q18", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(percentimproved)`% of users were more intellectually humble after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q18", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(tlab)`, Cohen's d = `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q18", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% slice(1) %>% pull(cohend) %>% round(2)`.

**Perspective-Taking**

We found that `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q4", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(percentimproved)`% of users scored higher on perspective-taking after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q4", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(tlab)`, Cohen's d = `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q4", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% slice(1) %>% pull(cohend) %>% round(2)`.


**Classroom Freedom of Expression**

We found that `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "C3") %>% pull(percentimproved)`% of users felt freer to express their beliefs in class after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "C3") %>% pull(tlab)`, Cohen's d = `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "C3") %>% slice(1) %>% pull(cohend) %>% round(2)`.

**Growth Mindset**

We found that `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q11", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(percentimproved)`% of users scored higher on Growth Mindset after completing OpenMind. Moreover, this improvement was statistically significant, `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q11", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(tlab)`, Cohen's d = `r basicsummarystats %>% filter(variable_code == "Q11", moderates == "WithModerates") %>% pull(cohend) %>% round(2)`.
